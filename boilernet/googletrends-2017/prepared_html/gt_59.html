    Magnum P.I.: "I Saw the Sun Rise" Review

    Share.
    Stache Grab.
    By Matt Fowler

    Warning: Spoilers for the Magnum P.I. Series Premiere follow...

    The original long-running Magnum P.I. series, which ran from '80 to '88, is known for a handful of things. First and foremost, it transformed Tom Selleck into a bonafide TV star (which he was even able to parlay into a few headlining movie roles) and, aside from the unique Hawaiian backdrop, it was Selleck's charm that kept viewers coming back for weekly capers. Add to the mix John Hillerman's Higgins - Thomas Magnum's snooty, feisty foil - and the series was able to cruise comfortably as a breezy "case of the week" procedural for many years.

    Decades later, CBS still builds shows using the same blueprint that TV adopted back in "the day." Drawing in an older demographic for its stable, stalwart lineup of "murder's the real star" mysteries, the network uses a time-honored regimented recipe for casual, lightly serialized (though mostly not) crook-buster shows, using tried and true tropes and cliches to present viewers with purposefully unchallenging storytelling.

    In this regard, the new Magnum is "next verse, same as the first." It fits in just fine on the Eye, which recently launched a reboot of '70s series S.W.A.T. and is currently prepping the ninth season of its Hawaii 5-O update (Magnum P.I. was originally created to make use of Hawaii 5-O's production team in Hawaii as that series wrapped, but - hey! - now these shows can exist in the same universe if needed). No, the lead, Jay Hernandez (Hostel, Crazy/Beautiful), isn't as charismatic as Selleck, but that's a big ask. No one really expected him to be and, honestly, CBS doesn't even need that in this day and age. It used to be that shows lived and died by the abject likability of the guy solving crimes, but now all that's required is a placeholder.

    Granted, Hernandez is more than a placeholder, but it's a hard buy-in to believe that his Magnum is equal parts blockbuster action hero and bungler. As Magnum, Hernandez is given all the surrounding hallmarks and trinkets of an affable screw up, but it doesn't gel quite right. That being said, he's a totally satisfactory surrogate centerpiece. Plus, who can resist, here in 2018, Magnum and Higgins becoming the "will they?/won't they?" couple? Of course, Higgins is now gender-swapped and played by Perdita Weeks (Penny Dreadful) but I spent a bulk of the episode thinking how much more interesting it would be if they kept Higgins as a man and stuck with the same flirtations.

    Obviously, Hernandez doesn't don the mustache, which some might argue is crucial to the character. I'd argue it's crucial to Tom Selleck, and Selleck's Magnum, and that it's not naturally needed here. It's become a "thing" either way.

    The opening episode is standard action-pilot fare - with a notable director subbing in (Fast and the Furious' Justin Lin), a beefed-up budget, and a highly personal case to tackle featuring a killed-off friend who will probably never be mentioned on the show again. Yes, if you know the old Magnum P.I. series then you're familiar with Magnum's friends T.C. and Rick. They're still here. T.C.'s the chopper pilot (now played by Stephen Hill) and Rick the connected man of the island (now Happy Endings' Zachary Knighton). Best bud Sebastian Nuzzo however? Not so notable.

    That's because Nuzzo (played by The Wire's Domenick Lombardozzi) does the honors of dying in the pilot, thus establishing the traditional series trio, and his murder fuels our first, fairly simply, adventure. Again, will anyone ever bring up Nuzzo again? Or will we ever again see his widow and son, who both Magnum and Higgins consoled and comforted during their dark hour? I'm betting not.

    Other elements of the old Magnum remain as well. Magnum lives on the estate of novelist Robin Masters, who uses old wartime exploits of Magnum and his buddies, with a touch of creative license, as characters for his best-selling Tom Clancy-type books. Higgins, who now does yoga while she tries to hide her past as an MI-6 operative, lords over the grounds with Dobermanns Zeus and Apollo. Even the title, "I Saw the Sunrise" is a callback to one of the original series' most famous episodes. It's a perfectly serviceable mix of old and new. Empty calories that pair well with any given weeknight lineup of murder-dunnits.

    Wrapping up here, I was particularly amused by the fact that Magnum, in the final action sequence of the episode, tries to do something crazy that's only described in Masters' books, but it fails - causing him to do something much more crazy and elaborate , stunt-wise, than the original move. But that's this new show, right? Magnum stumbles, but only in the coolest ways possible.

    The Verdict

    The new Magnum P.I. is a fast-moving, flighty field day that honors the original show's snappy spirit. Naturally, without Tom Selleck, or a lead who's able to match Selleck's charisma, the series is going to inherently be something else. Whether that's good or bad will be decided by history, but for now this is a popcorn procedural that slots in well on CBS.
